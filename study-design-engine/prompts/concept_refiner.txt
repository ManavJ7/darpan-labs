## Your Task
Refine the raw concept components into research-ready stimulus material. A concept board is a structured narrative that tells a story: Consumer Tension → Promise → Proof. Apply the following research-backed rules:

### Consumer Insight Framing Rules (the "Tension")
1. The insight MUST articulate a genuine consumer tension — a gap between what consumers want and what they currently experience in this specific category.
2. Frame it in the consumer's voice (e.g., "I want...", "I wish...", "It's frustrating that..."). Test: "Can you imagine a real person actually saying these words?"
3. The insight must be brand-agnostic — no brand mentions. It sets up a universal need that the product uniquely addresses.
4. Keep it to 2-3 sentences maximum. Mobile respondents need to grasp it instantly.
5. The tension must be specific to the category. For automotive: driving/ownership tensions. For tech: productivity/experience tensions. For FMCG: daily routine tensions. Avoid generic insights that could apply to any category.

### Single-Minded Benefit Rules (the "Promise")
1. ONE clear, differentiating benefit — not a laundry list. Research shows that concepts with a single benefit score higher on clarity and purchase intent than multi-benefit concepts.
2. Frame as an outcome the consumer experiences, not a product feature. "You'll feel confident on the road" not "Has 8 airbags."
3. The benefit MUST directly resolve the tension from the insight. If the insight is about safety anxiety, the benefit must address safety — not fuel efficiency.
4. The benefit must be believable and deliverable. Aspirational but not fantastical.

### Reasons to Believe (RTB) Credibility Chain (the "Proof")
1. Each RTB must follow: Feature → Mechanism → Consumer Outcome. Not just "uses AI" but "uses AI to analyze driving patterns → so the car adapts to your style → giving you a smoother, more intuitive drive."
2. Include 2-3 RTBs (never more than 4). Research shows more than 4 RTBs reduce believability.
3. RTBs must be concrete and specific — avoid vague claims like "advanced technology" or "premium quality."
4. At least one RTB should be verifiable (ingredient, patent, certification, clinical result, heritage).
5. RTBs must not contradict each other or the benefit.

### Concept Presentation Quality
- All language must be at a 6th-8th grade reading level. Consumer-friendly, no jargon.
- Product name should be memorable, category-appropriate, and distinct from {competitor_brands}.
- Visual description must be specific enough for a designer to execute but neutral enough to avoid biasing respondents (no aspirational lifestyle imagery that oversells).
- Price format should match category norms (per unit for FMCG, starting-from for automotive, monthly for subscriptions, etc.).
- Total concept should be readable in under 30 seconds on a mobile screen. If it takes longer, it's too long.

### Representativeness Check
- Only include elements consumers will actually experience in-market. If it wouldn't appear on packaging, in a showroom, or in a real ad, cut it.
- Testing non-representative stimuli produces non-predictive results.
