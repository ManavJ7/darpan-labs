## Your Task
Audit all the concepts above for comparability. In quantitative testing, every concept must be comparable in structure, tone, length, and level of finish. When concepts are not comparable, results are invalid — a better-written concept wins on execution quality, not idea strength.

## Seven Comparability Criteria

### 1. Level-of-Finish Parity
- All concepts must be at the same stage of development (all polished or all rough).
- Check: Are some concepts clearly more refined, detailed, or professional than others?
- Flag if any concept has significantly more or fewer RTBs, or a more/less developed visual description.
- This is the most common cause of invalid concept tests.

### 2. Benefit Clarity Equivalence
- Each concept's key benefit must be equally clear and comprehensible.
- Check: Can a consumer immediately grasp the main benefit of each concept in one read?
- Flag if any concept states multiple benefits while others state one (this biases in favor of the multi-benefit concept on appeal but against it on clarity).
- Flag if any concept's benefit is vague, compound, or overly technical compared to others.

### 3. Price Realism & Anchoring
- Pricing across concepts must be realistic for the category and not create anchoring bias.
- Check: Are all prices within a realistic range? Is the spread between highest and lowest price reasonable?
- Flag if any concept's price is an outlier. A concept at 2x the price of others will be evaluated differently regardless of idea quality.
- If this is not a price-testing study, consider whether prices should be included at all.

### 4. Loaded Language Detection
- No concept should use emotionally charged, aspirational, or superlative language that others lack.
- Check: Does any concept use words like "revolutionary", "amazing", "the best", "breakthrough", "premium quality" while others use neutral language?
- Flag any persuasive or marketing language that would unfairly bias respondent evaluation.
- The research standard: all concepts should read like objective descriptions, not sales pitches.

### 5. Structural Consistency
- All concepts must follow the same structural template (same sections, same order).
- Check: Does every concept have the same components present (insight, benefit, RTBs, visual, price)?
- Flag if any concept is missing a component or has additional sections that others lack.

### 6. Length Balance
- Word count across equivalent sections should be balanced (within 20% variance).
- Check: Compare the approximate word count of each component across all concepts.
- Flag if any concept's section is significantly longer or shorter than the same section in other concepts.
- Longer concepts tend to score higher on appeal simply because they provide more information.

### 7. Reading Level Consistency
- All concepts should be written at a comparable reading level.
- Check: Is one concept written in simple consumer language while another uses technical jargon or complex sentence structures?
- Flag if reading levels vary significantly across concepts.
